## **Welcome**

Hello, my name is Mateo and I will accompany you in this solution. If you are here is because you are interested in knowing how to take care and use those technological equipments that belong to your community or work team. In this case, when we talk about technological equipment we are referring to a computer, an antenna, a tablet, a data plan, etc. and to a material good that does not belong to anyone in particular, but to a community or group of people. 

💡 **Inspiration!** In **SOLE Colombia** we want more people to use the internet and technological equipment in groups, because it brings many benefits: - Using it together is more fun and invites us to share ideas - It is more economical and invites us to work as a team - When there is another person next to you is less likely to fall into malicious pages or cyber scams.

## What is using the game to learn to take care of teams in community?

People develop teamwork skills, learn to value collective responsibility and understand the importance of cooperating for the common good in a supportive environment. An excellent strategy to do this is through games and participatory activities.

## What is this solution for?

To begin with, you will be surprised if I tell you that we do not want to give you magic formulas on how to care for and use technological equipment in community, since we do not know the particularities of where you live and we understand that each context is different, but we do have a fun and deep game that will allow you to reach agreements with your community or work team called the **Fish Game**. With this game we hope that your community will reflect on the care, distribution and management of common resources on planet earth, while discussing how to reach agreements for the shared use of the equipment at your disposal. 

## **What do you need?**

1. You must have a bag of at least 80 candies, beans or tokens of the same size with which you will represent the fish in the game. 
2. A table.
3. Chairs surrounding the table.
4. Cardboard sheet size.
5. Markers.

## How to do it?

- **Moment 1: first game**
    
    
💡 For the **first game** place 50 of the 80 fish in the center of a table and tell the participants: "*There are 50 fish in a lake and you are fishermen/fisherwomen. To fish you must follow the following instructions:*
    
    *a. Each one can catch between 0 and 5 fish per round.*
    
    *b. You may not talk; you may only use your body language.*
    
    *c. Fish reproduce: 1 new fish is born for every 10 fish left available on the table after the round is over. Example: if there are 30 fish left on the tables, you must place three new fish.*
    
    *d. You decide when you finish the game."*

    
    After giving the instructions leave the bag with the 30 extra fish on the table and check that each person has his or her time to decide how many fish to take.
    
    At the end of the round ask the players to return the fish and ask the group:
    
    - How did they feel about the distribution of the fish, did they feel it was fair?

- **Moment 2: second game**
    
    
💡 For the **second game** the conditions change, so explain the following instructions out loud: "*Now there are 50 fish in a lake and you are still fishermen, the difference is that you are in a drought season, so fishing is much more difficult. There is a lot of scarcity and you must guarantee food to feed your families in the coming weeks.*" 
    **The rules of the game are the same. Mention the rules again to the participants if necessary.

    
    At the end of the round ask them: 
    
    - What has changed now? How do they feel about the distribution?
    - Do you think there might be similar difficulties when sharing the technological equipment in your community?
- **Moment 3: third game**
    
    
💡 For the **third game**, the situation is the same as the second game, but the instructions change: "*There are 50 fish in a lake and you are still fishermen, the difference is that it is the time of drought, so fishing is much more difficult. There is a lot of scarcity and you must guarantee food to feed your families for the next few weeks. There is not much time and so they must make quick decisions. " a. They can each catch between 0 and 5 fish per round. b. They can talk 2 minutes before fishing to decide how best to do it. c. Fish spawn: 1 new fish is born for every 10 fish that are available after the round is over. Example: if there are 30 fish left on the tables, you must put in three new fish. d. You decide when the game ends."*

    
    At the end of the round, ask the participants: 
    
    - Did the agreement you reached at the beginning of the game change anything?
    - Which of the games do you think had the fairest distribution? What differences are there among the three games?
    - How could the agreement they have made for the use and care of the technological equipment they have at their disposal be carried out?
- **Moment 4: the secret of reaching agreements**
    
    
💡 Tell people the secrets of the game to reach agreements:
    
    - The fishermen represent each of the people in your community.
    - The fish represent the finite resources or technological equipment that are available to the community, e.g., computers, internet antennas, data plans, etc. These fish also apply as an analogy about managing finite resources such as land, water, food or the air we breathe.
        
        The key to this game is that the more fish we catch for ourselves, the less fish will reproduce; so we all lose.
        
        Likewise, the less fish we catch, the more fish will reproduce on the table and the more resources the community will have.

- **Moment 5: let's reach an agreement**
    
    Give participants the poster board and markers. Invite them to write agreements on how to manage the resources or technological equipment they have at their disposal. We recommend downloading the following template to guide you in the conversation. 
    
    [Reglas uso y cuidado compartido de equipo tecnológicos.pptx](¿Cómo usar el juego para aprender a cuidar equipos 12a2bd68c5b6801a8d61de57be65d679/Reglas_uso_y_cuidado_compartido_de_equipo_tecnolgicos.pptx)
    
    These questions are only a guide, so you can add or remove questions as you see fit. Ask participants to write down the agreements they reached and invite them to share them with the rest of the community. This is essential because, in times of conflict or difficulties in solving a problem, they can go back to what they have written to come up with solutions or to rethink new agreements.
    

## **How does the game work?**

**First game:** in the first game people usually consume fish based on their interests and other people's behaviors. If one person consumes a lot of fish (5), other people tend to be motivated to consume the same amount; the same applies the other way around, if one person consumes few fish, other people (with some exceptions) tend to be motivated to consume less. Now, there are no magic formulas nor is the future written about what may happen, the key is to observe people's behaviors, as this will allow you to make powerful reflections on the responsible and communal management of finite resources. 

**Second game:** due to the conditioning of drought and the urgency to feed the family, in the second game people tend to consume more than in the first and third games, which causes the distribution of fish to be more inequitable and to run out faster. In other words, more tension is generated between personal benefit and common benefit. In behavioral psychology, this situation is called present bias: human beings, feeling that a resource is about to run out, activate a sense of loss that drives us to consume irrationally even when we do not need it. 

**Third game:** at this point, participants usually reach agreements by consuming less fish so that they reproduce more, preventing situations of scarcity and finding community alternatives for the equitable and fair management of resources; however, there is a possibility that this may not happen, the important thing is that you tell them the value of reaching agreements in the management of finite resources or technological equipment. When we reach agreements, all of us can benefit; on the contrary, when we all seek our personal benefits, the resources from which we can benefit tend to be destroyed, as well as our opportunity to continue to benefit from them.

## **How much is enough? The tragedy of the commons**

The tragedy of the commons is a problem about social relations and human behavior described by Garret Hardin in 1968 for Science magazine, in which he poses a thought experiment imagining a farmer who decides, on land shared with other farmers, to start putting more cows, since it generates more profit and personal benefits for him. The other farmers decide to do the same, so the field fills up with cows, the land is overexploited and deteriorates rapidly. Likewise, the farmers are left with many cows and no land to produce. This dilemma shows how we can destroy finite shared resources if each of us seeks only personal profit and invites us to think about the importance of reaching agreements about shared resources.
