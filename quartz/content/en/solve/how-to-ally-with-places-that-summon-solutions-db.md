# Partnering with others who are more visible

## Welcome

Hi, I'm Belen. I don't know much about technology, but I love people and building relationships with them is something I'm very good at. I came to SOLE some time ago and have learned a lot about the power of people and what we can achieve when we join with others. 

## What is it like to partner with places that convene?

There are multiple people and organizations that can make your message reach farther and clearer, that can make your projects a pathway to transformation in your community. Knowing how to reach them and get their attention is a way to join forces to achieve great things. 

## What is it for?

Many times you want to count on your community to do things, or learn together, and you find it difficult to find people to join the movement, or you end up doing things with the same people.

With these recommendations you can find out how to approach others to support the call in your community.

## What do you need?

- Courage
- Curiosity and listening skills
- Something to take notes with

## How to do it?

- **Moment 1: Define the purpose of the call, it will be easier to find allies.**
    
    The first thing is always the reason why you are going to do what you are going to do. If you intend to ally with others, you must be clear about the purpose of what you are going to do with them, only then, you will be able to focus the search for those who can help you to convene. 
    
    For example, if you are looking for people to build an antenna, would it be better to look for them in a hardware store, during the workday, or outside a kindergarten? Maybe it's a better place where people come to find materials to build things, or tinker. The owner of the hardware store may be a better ally than the school teachers for the call to build an antenna.
    
    Although you should not underestimate the talent of your community, you can prioritize the people and places you want to call with a clear 'why'. 
    
    If you can, define your proposal in detail: is it an event, or a series of events? what do you need to have, or buy, or what do you need to equip your purpose with? do you need more people, or are you going to do it on your own?
    
- **Moment 2: Locate the conveners, who they are and what they do.**
    
    Look around you: in your neighborhood, in your community, in your locality... maybe in your own home you have allies!
    
    Recognizing people and organizations with convening capacity, and allying ourselves with them, is strategic to achieve what we want. 
    
    Think about people and places where there is a flow of people, people who talk to the community, think about those who organize events. These types of actors will be relevant to convene... keep your purpose in mind so you can better see who has contact with the people you want to convene.
    
    It is worth asking ourselves how we can build networks to successfully convene our activities. If you work on consolidating your abilities to join forces with others, you will start to see how it flows better and better.

- **Moment 3: Read the interests of the conveners and how they resemble yours**
    
    Just as you want to organize a certain event, for example a SOLE, other people also have certain interests in the activities they want to promote.
    
    Think about what appeals to each of these actors and how you can serve their interests as well as your own: including the other in a negotiated agenda can win you a long-term ally, not just a momentary collaborator.
    
    For example, if your interest is to create the habit of doing SOLE in your community, you can partner with the Local Action Board. Understanding how using the Internet as a group to learn things together can meet the LAB's objectives related to planning and executing community development projects, or digital rights guarantees for the community, is a nice way to open up the conversation about the activity you want to organize and how the LAB can support the convening of your event.
    
- **Moment 4: Choose who you want to do what with**
    
    Now that you have recognized the strategic actors to convene, and you know how to align their objectives with the ones you have, choose the one you feel best to work with for each of the objectives you have.

- **Moment 5: Work on the invitation and the message you want to share.**
    
    Set out your ideas and prioritize them. Elaborate in depth each of the ideas you have and find the best way to present them to your ally is a way to practice the discourse and the way in which the information can better reach them.  
    
    You can make a script of possible questions and answers that may come up in the conversation. Put yourself in all possible scenarios, keep in mind the objections and problems your ally may have regarding your proposal. Having most of the scenarios contemplated, with a possible answer or alternative solution, will make your proposal look strong and consolidated. 
    
    Proposing a meeting to show your ideas in an orderly and calm manner is an excellent way to guarantee the attention of your potential ally. Agree with the person or persons who can give a 'yes' to your proposal, be punctual to that appointment and prepare the meeting well. 
    
    Work on the order in which you are going to present the information: a careful and orderly speech will let your interlocutor know that you are clear about what you want to do and will make him/her feel confident that he/she can count on you. 
    
    Don't forget to make it very clear 
    
    - what you are inviting your partner to do
    - what you will do and what you need from your partner
    - when you need them
    - for how long
    - who will be involved
    - how you will communicate with each other
    - how you will tell them what you have achieved because of the relationship between you.
- **Moment 6: Find out how you can make your proposal.**
    
    Some allies may be available to you anytime, anywhere. Others have a more formal way of operating, or need to make decisions by consensus. Still others need you to send a written proposal before you can talk to someone.
    
    Approach the person, agency, business, or other partner and find out the best way to present your proposal. Follow the instructions and organize your time to be able to respond promptly to the requirements. 
    
- **Step 7: Present your proposal and listen to your counterpart, the goal is to reach an agreement.**
    
    It's time to demonstrate everything you have worked for! Trust in yourself and in the rigor with which you have prepared your proposal, we are sure that at this moment, with all the information you have, you will find the words to tell a good story that will hook your ally to do things together. 
    
    Once you've made your point, listen carefully to your potential ally. Possibly, there's information you haven't contemplated, so take note of the points that are weak or need to be expanded upon. 
    
    There may also be a time for negotiation: you and your ally should have a win-win scenario. Open your eyes and ears to anything that might have value for your purpose and strive to build a solid relationship.
    
- **Moment 8: Formalize commitments and get to work!**
    
    The moment your potential ally is clear on everything and so are you, it's time to agree and make the next steps clear! 
    
    Write down what each of you is committed to and what the next steps are, it is always better not to forget anything.
    
    Thank the people who have taken care of you, starting to work together is just the beginning and celebrating the agreements is a way to honor this new relationship. 
    

## How do you know if this solution is working?

You'll know you've been successful in contacting places that convene the moment you start speaking the same language and find common ground in conversation. 

For example, when someone who can call others pays attention to the proposal you make and wants you to meet their manager, or a colleague who can help you call people. Or also, when you find yourself adapting what you are going to say to the interest of the person who is listening to you, creating a space where the ideas start to belong to both of you. 

If you exercise your listening skills, very soon you will be recognizing people with the ability to convene and seek to interest them. 

If your purpose is to SOLE, you can start thinking of your Self-Organized Learning Space as a place where several allies can start to fulfill their purposes thanks to your space, or, the other way around, you can think of your ally's space as a SOLE and benefit the people your ally convenes to learn to use the Internet as a group to live better together. 

You can SOLE any activity or space!

## Why would this solution work?

These recommendations are simple and limited. When you go looking for allies, much will depend on your communication skills.

Remember that you will not always be able to convince everyone to work with you, the important thing is to do it many times: 'experience makes perfect'. And, in turn, listen to your intuition to start creating a 'radar' in you that will alert you to the people who can be your allies... be attentive!
