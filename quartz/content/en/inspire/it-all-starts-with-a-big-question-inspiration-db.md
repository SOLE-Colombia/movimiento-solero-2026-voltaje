- **Read the transcript**
    
    Historically, if you want to know something, the traditional thing was to find someone who knew it and ask them. That's not very practical because you can't always find the person in question or you can't always go see them, so it used to take a long time to learn things. That all changed because of writing. People could write down what they knew and, if you wanted to know, you could find it and read it.
    
    But some people were opposed to writing. Surprisingly, the most famous of those who opposed writing was Socrates, the Greek philosopher. His objection was that you can read what someone has written, but you can't ask them questions, and sometimes it's not easy to find what they've written either. That problem was solved by libraries, where you keep the writings of many people in one place, but even that had a problem because, first of all, you have to find the library and go to it. Secondly, if you're reading something and you have a question, you have no one to ask.
    
    That started to change thanks to the Internet. Now, you can find what anyone writes at any time and, while reading, if you have a question, you can (usually) find someone to ask. So, the two problems Socrates had, namely 1) where do I find the writings and 2) who do I ask if I have a question, seem to have been solved thanks to the Internet.
    
    To this we must add one last thing: if the reading is done with many people together, each one can ask the other. Therefore, a group of learners can build a very inexpensive place with little material available where they have a computer, Internet and can ask questions to each other. In other words, it is very easy to create a learning environment by yourself and that is what we call a Self-Organized Learning Environment or SOLE.
    
    The idea is very simple and powerful: it is possible for learners to create their own learning environment using the Internet! 
    

## So, if you wanted to know the answer to something you don't know, how would you do it?

Think of anyone, like you, young or old, anywhere in the world who wants to answer a question they have always had and have never been able to solve: their possibilities for learning, from the very old to the very new, could be: 

### 1. Ask someone who knows: Oral tradition 2.

Find someone around you who knows the answer to your Big Question. It could be a friend, your parents or grandparents, a teacher, or a neighbor. 

If they don't have the answer, ask them who might have it and travel together with this question to ask that other person. 

This is how most ancestral knowledge is passed on, even today.  

### 2. Search in writings: Textual transmission

Where are there writings on the subject of this question: at home, at a neighbor's house, at school, at the public library, or somewhere in the nearest town?

Go and look for them together. As in the history of libraries, knowledge can be passed on in writing and librarians or teachers may know what books your answer may be in.

Imagine... This is how we have learned since writing was invented, and it has gradually become more equitable, as public education becomes more universal. Yet there are many people who still do not have access to it.

    Great! And did you have any new questions? Go back and **ask someone who knows**, or go back and **look at the writings** you have at your fingertips.

    So where else can we find it? I guess if you're reading this, it may sound obvious that you can **search the Internet**. Just keep in mind that, in 2024, half the people in the world will NOT have access to the Internet. 
    

### 3. Searching the Internet: Digital Interconnectedness

Searching the Internet is like having the world's largest library in your pocket. Google or any other search engine, where you type your Big Question is like the librarian that searches through all the texts, videos, audios and other content on the Internet and offers it to you so you can explore it. 

However, I don't know if you know, but searching the Internet on your own is difficult. There are many answers to your Big Questions, some are better than others, but there are also many wrong ones. There are even lies written by people or even machines, like the ones they call Artificial Intelligence (AI). Learning to judge that sea of information in order to learn is quite an art. The same thing happens when you ask someone who knows or search in writings. 

The best way to overcome this is to **doubt, be curious and ask new questions**. This is what scientists and artists do, they don't stick with the first thing they learn. Although sometimes learning alone - being self-taught - is demanding, regardless of whether you have access to all the information. 

### 4. Search the internet in a group: SOLEar

At SOLE Colombia we have found that many people who do not have access to good education can learn to learn almost anything using the internet in a group because they can doubt, be curious and ask new questions together. It is like being self-taught but in a group which is not only easier, funnier and more powerful than using the internet alone, but also proposes a new way of being together. We call this SOLEar.  

![Imagen](https:/www.notion.so/icons/new-alert_green.svg)

**WHAT IS SOLE?** 

SOLE (Self-Organized Learning Environments) is a disruptive learning methodology that empowers communities to develop autonomy, collaboration, deep learning and, most importantly of all, to solve real-life problems! 

A SOLE session is simple. You need a space with people, few computers with internet connection and Great Questions. In a SOLE session learners self-organize to find answers to questions using the Internet, without the need for a teacher.

**WHAT IS THE GRANNY CLOUD?**

Along with SOLE is the concept of the Granny Cloud. Research has shown that the limited intervention of an untrained facilitator can promote successful learning processes. The role of a Abuelita is to provoke curiosity, ask questions, listen attentively and provide a warm environment. The Abuelita Cloud is a network of volunteers from all walks of life who connect remotely with SOLEs to co-facilitate and offer participants the opportunity to experience worlds far removed from their own.

## What if you don't have internet?

If you don't have internet, you will always be able to learn more using the first options even if they have the difficulties Sugata Mitra mentions in the video. 

However, at SOLE Colombia 

At SOLE Voltaje we have the idea that using the Internet in groups to learn is a way to have new healthy human relationships that will not only change the way we learn and dialogue but also the way we create the world we want together. Moreover, it will change the shape of the Internet to be more inclusive, diverse and equitable. But for this to happen, it is important that everyone has access to the Internet.
