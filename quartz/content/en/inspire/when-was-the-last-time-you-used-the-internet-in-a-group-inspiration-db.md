So, let's go back to the beginning: when was the last time you used the Internet in a group? Perhaps that memory—sharing a search, a screen, a question—is the beginning of another possible Internet. The Library of the Future is just that: proof that other Internets are possible, and that we are already creating them.

![Recarga Tu SOLE Lab, El Tambo, Cauca.](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60ecebdfd98c8137563c01c1/68a3c61cd753633846d1d9c4_20240801_SOLE+BOLT_SOLELAB_Enchufa_ElTambo_  (235).jpeg)

Recarga Tu SOLE Lab, El Tambo, Cauca.

That's what we're creating with The Library of the Future: an ecosystem where access isn't the end, but the starting point. A space where the network not only connects devices, but also stories, doubts, and intuitions—a space for us to connect and ask questions without fear.

In SOLE Labs, we've seen how, when a community gathers around a screen, something changes. Girls, young people, and adults asking questions together. The internet ceases to be a flow of information we consume alone and becomes a shared territory. In groups, questions arise that don't arise when we're alone. Memories merge, curiosity is awakened, and collective projects are born. Using the internet in a group is, in itself, a radical act because it challenges speed, noise, and the instruction to accept without question.

![Enchufa tu SOLE Lab en Dominguillo, Cauca.](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60ecebdfd98c8137563c01c1/687d8c39e4870485f4ee9ff6_20241020_SOLE+BOLT_Enchufa_Dominguillo (157).jpg)

Enchufa tu SOLE Lab en Dominguillo, Cauca.

The internet has become a personal space for consumption; "being connected" doesn't really mean being connected to others or connecting with others. And that's what changes when we use the internet in groups.

![Festival SOLE en Boca de Camarones, la Guajira.](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/60ecebdfd98c8137563c01c1/68a3c5ac0b48461f60decf79_20240419_SOLE+BOLT_BocaDeCamarones_(88).jpg)

Festival SOLE en Boca de Camarones, la Guajira.

Today, nearly three billion people in the world remain disconnected. And this digital exclusion cuts across bodies, languages, and landscapes. It's not just a technical problem: it's a silent way of saying who counts and who doesn't. But that's not all. Those of us who have access to the internet tend to use it not to connect, but to further isolate ourselves from those around us. We use it to order food, to learn about other people's lives, to buy things, to read about what interests us. We rarely use it to cook with others, to meet with others, to exchange knowledge, to create together.

Perhaps you don't remember this. Because most of the time we connect alone: in front of a cell phone, a laptop, or a tablet. The internet experience has become intimate, individual, and often passive. We accept answers that appear without asking, we navigate algorithms that predict what we want before we even say it. But there's something that's lost when we use the internet alone: the possibility of looking into each other's eyes while searching, of asking ourselves questions out loud, of inventing together.

### When was the last time you used the internet in a group?

## **While millions remain without internet access, those of us who have access use it individually. What if the future of the internet is to use it in groups?**
